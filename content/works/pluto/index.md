冥王星──它暗示著被排除的軌道、失效的定義與冷落的存在。冥王星總是離太陽太遠，也不太符合行星的「標準定義」，所以被劃為「矮行星」，它的運行像是某種不被預期的軌道。而這件作品中的觀看者，也被迫站在這種偏移的、無法中心對位的位置上。裝置中的圖像對觀者來說是模糊的、破碎的，只能被部分觀看——這些破碎的圖像殘片，與其說是要「被讀取」，不如說是要「被迷失」。
這種被驅逐的命名策略，不只是對天文學分類的戲謔，也隱含著觀看位置的邊緣化：觀者永遠在外緣、在遠距離的環繞中窺視，而無法佔據那個被設計為「核心」的凝視點。若從作品現場的裝置結構觀察，《冥王星》的觀看方式近乎被導引至某種擬劇場的邏輯。觀者進入空間，即被那些紙質人像、碎裂塑件與銀色構件包圍在一個多層次的觀看路徑中。中央有一組仿似擬人形或機械內臟的主體結構，其上覆蓋著投影圖樣、彩繪殘像與金屬表層；四周分布著紙製的巨大面孔、過度上色的人像模件，以及如舊電子產品般的裝置殘軀。這些物件彼此指涉，卻無統一敘事；它們並非指向某個表演，而更像是某場演出之後被遺棄的殘留場景。

圖像內容本身延續了我過去對低階文化的關注。我在《冥王星》中引入了許多近似剝削電影（exploitation films）的，來自網路的影像碎片。這些被撿拾回來的素材——斷裂的眼神、過曝的肉體、過度渲染的血跡與光影——不再指向任何敘事，它們只是圖像過剩的餘燼。在布希亞的擬像體系中，這些圖像已不再仿真某個真實，而是僅僅指涉其自身的複製性（Baudrillard, 1994）。在這種條件下，我選擇將它們進一步置入紙製裝置的表面，不是為了還原某種風格或戲劇性，而是讓這些圖像成為可疑的附著物，一種無處可去的觀感殘渣。



Pluto suggests an excluded orbit, a failed definition, and a neglected existence. Always too distant from the Sun and unable to meet the “standard” criteria of a planet, Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet, moving along an unexpected trajectory. In this work, the viewer is likewise forced into a displaced position—unable to align with any stable center. The images within the installation appear blurred and fragmented, accessible only in parts. These fragments are not meant to be read so much as lost within.

This strategy of expulsion is not merely a playful critique of astronomical classification; it also implies the marginalization of the viewing position itself. The viewer remains on the periphery, circling at a distance, never occupying the gaze point designed as the “center.” Observed through its spatial configuration, Pluto guides viewing toward a quasi-theatrical logic. Upon entering the space, the viewer is enveloped by layered paths of perception—paper figures, fractured plastic components, and silver structural elements. At the center stands a core form resembling a humanoid body or mechanical organs, covered with projected patterns, painted residues, and metallic surfaces. Surrounding it are oversized paper faces, over-colored figurative modules, and the remnants of devices reminiscent of obsolete electronics. These objects refer to one another without forming a unified narrative; rather than pointing to a performance, they resemble the remains of a scene abandoned after the event.

The imagery continues my ongoing engagement with low-grade or marginal cultural forms. In Pluto, I incorporate internet-sourced fragments akin to exploitation films. These salvaged materials—disjointed gazes, overexposed bodies, excessively rendered blood and lighting—no longer serve narrative ends; they are embers of image excess. Within Baudrillard’s system of simulacra, such images no longer imitate a reality but signify only their own reproducibility (Baudrillard, 1994). Under these conditions, I embed them into the surfaces of the paper installation—not to reconstruct a style or dramatize content, but to let them persist as dubious attachments: perceptual residues with nowhere else to go.
